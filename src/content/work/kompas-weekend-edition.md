## The problem

The weekend supplements were the paper's showcase — travel, culture, young readers —
but each was laid out from scratch every week by whoever was on shift. No shared grid,
five headline typefaces, and infographics drawn per-article. Production regularly ran
past the print deadline, and the sections looked like three different publications.

## The process

This was my first job out of school, and it taught me what a system is *for*. Working
under the art director, I audited three months of published pages, then built a single
modular grid (12 columns, 6 row units) that all three supplements could share, with a
reduced type palette of two families and a fixed hierarchy. I designed a library of
infographic components — maps, timelines, step diagrams — as InDesign assets that
editors could drop in and restyle without breaking the system.

The hard part wasn't the grid; it was adoption. I ran layout workshops for the desk
and turned the rules into a one-page cheat sheet taped next to every workstation.

## The outcome

Layout production time fell by roughly 30%, and the weekend editions finally shipped
on deadline every week of the following quarter. Newsstand pickup for the weekend
edition rose 18% year-on-year after the refresh. A decade later, I still design
interfaces the way that newsroom taught me: grid first, hierarchy second, decoration
last.
